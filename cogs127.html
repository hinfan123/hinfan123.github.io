Background

Project Objectives

We wanted to redesign a feature in an already existing banking application to help users save for major upcoming high-cost purchases.

Our team hopes to provide a more time efficient and simple experience when saving money.

Target Users

Our target users are young adults
Here are our reasons why.

Coming of age

Young adults are just now getting into the real world and have to start saving for many different things (rent, car payments, loans).

Environment

People often hang around others of the same age, young adults spending time together almost always results in spending money.

User Goals

Users (young adults) should be able to

  • set goal amounts of how much they want to save up
  • strategize what things they should and shouldn't spend money on based on their current spending habits
  • have mentality that money set aside is not money lost

    • Problem Statement

    Many young adults are not fully aware of their spending habits or saving methods and are unable to save correctly when saving up for emergencies.

    Personas

    Craig

    About: Craig is a sports enthusiast and enjoys playing rugby on his weekends. He knows its a dangerous sport and has had emergencies before so he wants to be able to save up and not have to worry about accidents that might occur.

    Paint Points: Bills for accidents vary greatly from the cost of treating a sprained ankle to the cost of treating broken limbs. Craig is not very tech savvy and finds it difficult to constantly input expenses into his phone and set up saving features on mobile apps.

    Greg

    About: Greg has recently adopted a pet dog. He is pretty sure that he can afford the costs of raising a pet, but wants to save up money for an emergency fund to cover vetinarian bills in case his dog falls ill or has an accident.

    Paint Points: Greg isn’t really sure how much money he needs to set aside as an emergency fund.

    He has had many unexpected purchases recently as a result of getting a new pet, and finds it difficult to keep track of how much money he has left at the end of the month to put towards his new savings.

    Sarah

    About: Sarah is a recent college graduate with a new job. She is unsure of how long she is going to need to repay her loans and wants to save up money so that she will still be able to pay her loans even in emergencies.

    Paint Points: Student loans, when unpaid, can rack up interest. It’s difficult to make time and account for all of her expenses between work and getting used to adult life.

    UX Flows

    UX Flow A Images

    This user flow demonstrates one method of implementing microsavings by rounding up on specific individual purchases. This gives the user more control over which transactions to round or not. Users such as Greg, who may not be able to contribute a set amount from month to month, can then pick and choose which transactions to round up. Furthermore, in order to streamline the process of rounding individual transactions, there are simplified options and no confusing changing of settings. The simple yes/no choices are easy to understand even for users such as Craig who are not tech savvy and might have trouble accessing and changing things in the settings menu

    UX Flow B Images

    This user flow demonstrates a method by which Sarah can manage interest from her loans more concretely. She begins by selecting the account which contains the balance she wants to keep track of, bringing up a screen showing her current balance along with what that balance will be at in (some point in time; maybe a year? 3 years?) if she were to keep on paying the minimum balance. Sarah decides that she wants to pay more than the minimum, so she elects to change up the amount she is paying per month. After seeing the change in how much she needs to pay over time, Sarah is satisfied, and applies this change to her current budget. By doing this, Sarah is able to more easily remain cognizant of the amount that she has save up for in order to reduce debt, making it easier for her to make larger purchases in the future.

    UI Sketches

    UI Sketches Images

    Our team learned that making UI Sketches (paper prototyping) is very tedious and long, but it allows us to connect our UX Flow sheets to our sketches through the step by step process when using the application. Paper prototyping also allows for making multiple iterations of layouts, and oftentimes we would come up with new, better ideas while in the midst of sketching our intital idea. We wanted to add the rounding setting so that the application automatically rounded up when categorizing the transactions, and our paper prototypes explored placing that feature directly into the transactions screen as well as having it as a toggled setting. Our sketches took from Acorn’s round up feature and altered it to save the money rather than invest that money so the user can use it to fund anything else they might need, as well as Digit’s savings account feature for the interest payoff plans. We also borrowed inspiration from popular apps such as Tinder that use a more intuitive left/right swiping motion to access screens instead of having users have to go through multiple decision-making screens.

    Paper Prototypes

    Paper Prototype A Images

    Our design for our first prototype `is based off of the first UX flow, which allows users to “save their digital change” by rounding up on transactions and placing these small amounts of money into a savings account. The idea behind it is that as the user spends money, they also simultaneously have an easy method of saving. In A2 we also explored the idea of automatic savings, but a) many banks already offer this and it would be redundant to replicate it on a mobile app since it is a one time action as well as b) not all users have a fixed monthly income and might want to save a variable amount each month. Thus, in order to make saving as painless a process as possible when having to enter savings manually, rounding up on individual purchases allows enough flexibility in saving amounts while still being easily accessible. Similarly, the swiping mechanism to round up transactions minimizes the number of steps one needs to take. Users need as little as two or at most three swipes/taps to immediately put money into their savings.

    Paper Prototype B Images

    We designed our second prototype based off of our second UX flow to allow users to edit how much they want to pay off their debt (in this case, their credit card debt). We kept the idea of changing the amount they want to pay off each month with the goal of paying off their card in a certain amount of time. Instead of adding more buttons to press when showing their budget over time, we used a modal to show how long it would take to pay off their debt as they change their monthly payment. This way, users get an idea of how much they need to save so they can pay off their debt. The differences in our prototypes was the type of interaction the user had with the application. With Prototype A, the user was more focused on saving money by rounding their spent money so that the app puts away a certain amount every time they spend. With Prototype B, the user is more focused on paying back spent money (debt) so that they are aware of how much they need to pay and set a schedule for payments.

    User Testing

    User Testing Images

    We tested our prototypes on three UCSD students. For each of the three, they began by being debriefed about the overall type of app we are using and were then given a brief overview of the task they were supposed to complete: for the microsavings prototype, they were instructed to choose a purchase and round up three dollars on a purchase, and for the interest payoff prototype, they were instructed to change the minimum payment on their credit card. After running through both prototypes, each user was asked what their biggest issue was and which prototype they preferred. Each user managed to complete these tasks with little outside assistance aside from one portion of the second prototype.

    High-Fidelity Prototypes V1

    Main Prototype Flow Images

    This is our original design that follows the paper prototype we created that implemented one of our original UX flows. We decided to proceed with this prototype over the other after conducting three interviews in which users tested out two different UX flows, and all three users prefered the experience of the prototype we implemented over the other one. Here, we utilize arrows as signifiers to make the user swipe to the right, opening up a new menu that would allow for them to round up a purchase. We believe that by having the arrows pointing to the right, a user would attempt to swipe to the right. After a purchase is rounded, we then remove the arrow by that transaction, helping the user remember the transactions they’ve rounded already

    Alternative Screen Images

    We decided to create another whole flow that will act as our alternate screens because we felt that we should have a different action that brings us to the desired event while still following the flow of our paper prototype. Additionally, this design is more akin to the original Mint app as the android version has a hold down function while the IOS version does not. These screens are testing a different type of user interaction to get to the rounding (holding down versus swiping). We believe that holding down is another common action many mobile users perform and is quite intuitive because the transaction component will become highlighted onced the user touches that component.

    High-Fidelity Prototypes V2

    Prototype Flows and before-after Images

    Based on user feedback from our friends and classmates, we decided to use the tap-and-hold interaction over the swiping interaction to implement the rounding up feature. This is mainly because with the tapping feature, the transaction remains visible for more of the rounding process so that the user remains well informed about which transaction they are rounding on. With the swiping action, the transaction is hidden and if a user wants to double check which transaction they are rounding on they must cancel the entire process to view the transaction again. Holding down on a touch target is also a more purposeful action that prevents users from accidentally rounding transactions through careless handling of their phone. Although swiping is the quicker and more intuitive action, it also leaves more room for error and for users to accidentally swipe on the wrong thing. Swiping works well for more low-stakes apps such as Tinder, but with an app like Mint that deals with money, users may prefer an interaction that is slower but more precise.

    We also made a few logistical changes to the wording of the dialog on the feature. Rather than having the text read “Round Up By”, we switched it to “Round Up To”, which saves the user from having to do mental math to add together the transaction and the change. Instead, the user can think of the total value that the transaction has been rounded to as the new value of the transaction. In order to help the user see how much they are saving on each rounding action and save them the trouble of calculating it, we kept the ending notification dialog as “$___ has been added to your savings account!” in order to inform the user on not only the total rounded value but also the amount being saved.

    Finally, we changed the numerical values of the rounding feature, based on user feedback that rounding by multiples of change felt too small and that they would like the option to save faster by rounding higher. Thus, instead of rounding to the nearest dollar and having multiples of that number, we chose to have options rounding to the nearest 50 cents, the nearest dollar, and for larger transactions, to the nearest $10 or even nearest $100. This allows the user to save in increments as small or large as they want, and can also slightly tailor the app to users in different financial situations. A user that often makes large purchases that costs $50 or $100 will probably want to save larger amounts since they have the means to spend larger amounts, while maybe a college student that only makes purchases under $20 would like to save smaller amounts since they do not have as much income to set aside into savings.
