Overview

It’s ironic how it’s more difficult to find friends in today’s day and age when we are historically more connected then we have ever been. AFriend was created as a social computing system to explore how might we leverage geolocation and anonymity to create an opportunity for strangers to become AFriend to each other.

We primarily prototyped AFriend at UC San Diego using our library as a hotspot to have data-driven designs.

Problem

"The major need we identified was the barrier of establishing genuine connections with others and the fear of social repercussions when committing to creating new friendships."

Research

We conducted online surveys and in person interviews to try and get a better sense of how people use social media. We then asked about the impact of anonymity online and coupled it with our data. One of the interviewees asked “How long would it take for people to go the next step and introduce each other, and what would the possibilty be of actually meeting in person". We chose to make those questions our main points of focus.

From our interviews and surveys, we found that 6/10 users do not take online interactions too seriously and that the anonymity aspect addresses the fear of social reprecussions so people could unapologetically be themselves for the good and the bad.

Furthermore, 7/10 interviewees were interested in the idea of starting off anonymous and then revealing themselves if their online interactions went well.

Ideation

We used the concept of a localized chat based on your location. We then wanted to match users with each others based on common interests. A user would join a local chat room and see the list of everyone in that area and their match rating compared to other's next to their "names".

We planned to then implement a “wave” feature to notify other's that someone would like to chat with them privately. Another feature would be to "roll the dice" and get paired wtih a random person that has a high match rating with yours.

Prototyping

We used discord to represent the online messaging features of our prototype and we discovered that the features discord already has would mesh well with what the final product of our application would hold.

However, since we didn't moderate the chats carefully enough, content that people could find offensive did slip into the conversations.

In addition to this, we noticed that the audience did not move around to change rooms, so future iterations might include things that incentivize moving from one location to another.

Evaluations

Final Designs

Each chat room would be based on a physical location. Users may only join nearby locations to encourage interaction online and potentially offline.

Users would able to see chatrooms and activity within a certain range, but cannot participate until they are within a smaller range dependent on the location. Incentivizes users to move to locations and be in the same physical space as other users, and discourage randoms from “trolling” or harassment.

Each chat room would be based on a physical location. Users may only join nearby locations to encourage interaction online and potentially create opportunities to meet offline.

Individual chats would only between 2 people and would allow for a more intimate conversation. Users would indicate in their preferences whether or not they would allow the system to automatically match them users with similar interests.

Takeaway

Using geolocation allows users to interact with each other while they are in the same place at the same time.

Messaging is difficult to manage as moderating group chats requires a lot of work and if not moderated well, can scare off potential users.

The best use cases to pivot towards would be for users to use this application during class to help each other with notes or get class updates. They can then create study groups based off who they enjoyed talking to.
