Overview

I researched and designed a new feature for the banking application, Mint. Mint is a mobile application that connects to your bank account and provides a variety of ways to help budget money. The goal was to implement a more intuitive way to save money that allows new users to stay motivated to save money.

Throughout this project, my main focuses were research, finding user needs, creating UX Flows, paper prototyping, and high-fidelity prototyping.

Problem

Many young adults are constantly facing new financial problems whether it be paying off debt, car insurance, or even paying off a house. In doing so, many have trouble finding efficient ways to save money and are often scared away from saving in the first place because the entire process of budgeting and saving money can be overwhelming.

Research

The research we conducted aimed to identify the main banking applications people used, the reason why use those applications, and the techniques practiced to save money.

With that understanding, we set off to interview potential users. We asked these two questions.

1) What do you currently use to budget your money?
2) How do you keep track of your money through those applications?

Dat Nguyen

Age: 22

“I use Chase as my banking application, but I don’t really use it’s budgeting features because I get too lazy to manage everything.”

“For the most part, I usually keep track of everything I spend through my bank statements and in my head. If I need to save up for something, I just make sure to spend less.”

Key Takeaway

Users sometimes have difficulty using applications because of the barrier to entry and the amount of work added onto everyday management can be time consuming.

Kat Thongnoppakun

Age: 23

“I like Mint more than most other apps just because of the simple interface and the fact that it connects to my bank account.”

“I don’t really use it to save money, it’s more for keeping track of my spending so that I don’t overspend since I splurge a lot.”

Key Takeaway

Users typically go onto these applications to check or visualize their spending habits rather than to set goals and follow certain criteria. It’s a much more loose way of saving up money.

Personas

Craig

About: Craig is a sports enthusiast and enjoys playing rugby on his weekends. He knows its a dangerous sport and has had emergencies before so he wants to be able to save up and not have to worry about accidents that might occur.

Paint Points: Bills for accidents vary greatly from the cost of treating a sprained ankle to the cost of treating broken limbs. Craig is not very tech savvy and finds it difficult to constantly input expenses into his phone and set up saving features on mobile apps.

Greg

About: Greg has recently adopted a pet dog. He is pretty sure that he can afford the costs of raising a pet, but wants to save up money for an emergency fund to cover vetinarian bills in case his dog falls ill or has an accident.

Paint Points: Greg isn’t really sure how much money he needs to set aside as an emergency fund.

Sarah

About: Sarah is a recent college graduate with a new job. She is unsure of how long she is going to need to repay her loans and wants to save up money so that she will still be able to pay her loans even in emergencies.

Paint Points: Student loans, when unpaid, can rack up interest. It’s difficult to make time and account for all of her expenses between work and getting used to adult life.

User Flow

Paper Prototypes

Our design for our prototype allows users to “save their digital change” by rounding up on transactions and placing these small amounts of money into a savings account.

We explored the idea of automatic savings, but many banks already offer this and it would be redundant to replicate it on a mobile app since it is a one time action not all users have a fixed monthly income and might want to save a variable amount each month.

Thus, in order to make saving as painless a process as possible when having to enter savings manually, rounding up on individual purchases allows enough flexibility in saving amounts while still being easily accessible.

User Testing

We tested our prototypes on three UCSD students. After running through the prototype, each user was asked for the things they liked and disliked.

Each user managed to complete these tasks with little outside assistance aside from one portion of the second prototype.

High-Fidelity Prototypes

Original (Swipe Right)

This is our original design that follows the paper prototype we created that implemented our original UX flow. Here, we utilize arrows as signifiers to make the user swipe to the right, opening up a new menu that would allow for them to round up a purchase.

Alternate (Hold Down)

We went with a different action to indicate the new savings features. In this one, we chose to hold down the transaction which will then prompt the amount they want to save.

Final Designs

Based on user feedback from our friends and classmates, we decided to use the tap-and-hold interaction over the swiping interaction to implement the rounding up feature. Holding down on a touch target is also a more purposeful action that prevents users from accidentally rounding transactions through careless handling of their phone.

Additionally, we changed the numerical values of the rounding feature, based on user feedback that rounding by multiples of change felt too small and that they would like the option to save faster by rounding higher. Thus, instead of rounding to the nearest dollar and having multiples of that number, we chose to have options rounding to the nearest 50 cents, the nearest dollar, and for larger transactions, to the nearest $10 or even nearest $100.

Reflection

This case study provided great insight on current applications in the market. It's interesting to see how, with the right research and design foundations, we can potentially improve current applications that already feel complete.

I feel that our idea is more of a stepping stone rather than a replacemnet. It's simplicity and efficiency should motivate new users to actually start saving money and eventually branch out to other money saving features that are more complex.

Our goal was to create something that was extremely easy to use so that new users won't be intimated or overwhelmed and I believe that we succeeded.
